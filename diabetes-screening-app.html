A blood-work screening tool for undiagnosed Type-2 Diabetes

ENG1810 · Project Report

Abstract

Type-2 Diabetes is a serious, chronic disease characterised by elevated blood glucose levels. It is a prevalent and growing problem within Australia, with over 1.3 million diagnosed cases. Whilst diabetes can be easily identified and diagnosed via explicit HbA1c testing at a local doctor or medical centre, there are still over 500,000 people in Australia who are living with undiagnosed diabetes. Undiagnosed cases within Australia remain unidentified for one key reason: people who are not actively worried about diabetes will not test for it. This stems from a social stigma underlying the condition involving negative judgement from others, blame and stereotyping, as well as a lack of knowledge about early-stage diabetes, which is often physically undetectable and only detectable within blood-work.

To address this real-world problem, the project report seeks to address two key questions:

01How can computational analysis be used to reduce the number of unidentified cases?
02How can Australians be incentivised to undergo testing for Type-2 Diabetes?

In addressing this real-world problem, this project applies and develops a diabetes risk-screening tool that analyses general blood-work data collected within routine GP check-ups and pre-employment medicals. Rather than depending on individuals to recognise their own diabetes risk and independently request diabetes-specific testing, the project analyses existing blood-work data that is collected more frequently. In doing so, it acts as an early flagging mechanism for individuals who may require further HbA1c testing or medical assessments, providing a range of quantitative risk metrics.

Scope. It does not function as a diagnostic tool, does not replace HbA1c testing, and does not distinguish between Type-1 and Type-2 diabetes risk. It is a suggestive risk-flagging tool for Type-2 Diabetes specifically.

The significance of the intended solution addresses the two key questions by flagging individuals who considered themselves not at risk for further testing and increasing the rate of HbA1c testing. The facilitation of early detection has the potential to reduce preventable harm and long-term healthcare burden, not only improving the overall health of Australian citizens, but also reducing stress on Australia's healthcare system, currently costing over $9.1 billion annually in diabetes treatment and mitigation.

In considering this project and its potential application within the real world, this tool offers a unique value proposition compared to existing Australian screening tools, such as AUSDRISK. Rather than relying on lifestyle and demographic information, it instead utilises blood-specific data already collected through general blood check-ups. This makes it more clinically grounded and scientifically driven, as it can identify diabetes risk using measurable biological indicators. Furthermore, the data that can be extracted from this software, identifying changes in certain bloodwork factors over time of the general Australian population, is highly valuable.

What does the code do?

The code uses dynamic algorithmic modelling to create a predictive diabetes risk-screening tool. A pre-existing dataset of approximately 1000 individuals is input into the software, containing general bloodwork data and a confirmed diabetes diagnosis for each individual, where 0 represents non-diabetic and 1 represents diabetic.

The software analyses this dataset to identify the factors most strongly correlated with diabetes. As new general check-up data is entered, the program assigns each individual a predictive risk score based on their bloodwork values. Once an individual's diagnosis is later confirmed, this result is added back into the dataset, allowing the algorithm to recalibrate and improve the accuracy of its scoring system over time.

This system could be easily integrated into existing blood check-ups in Australia, as these bloodwork values are already commonly collected and digitised.

The maths behind it all

The tool draws on several statistical ideas. None of them require the reader to follow the underlying algebra — what matters is the role each plays in turning a column of blood results into a trustworthy risk signal.

Correlation and weighting

The model first asks, for every bloodwork marker, how cleanly it separates the people who turned out to be diabetic from those who did not. Markers that pull the two groups far apart are treated as more informative and given more influence over the final score; markers that barely differ between the groups are allowed to fade into the background. This is what lets the tool decide, from the data alone, which parts of a blood panel are worth paying attention to — and that decision is revisited every time a new result arrives.

Covariance between factors

Bloodwork markers do not move independently. Total cholesterol, LDL, HDL and triglycerides rise and fall together; metabolic markers such as BMI, triglycerides, liver enzymes and uric acid tend to cluster; kidney measures track one another. If every marker were simply added up on its own, these overlaps would be counted several times over and the score would be exaggerated. Measuring the covariance between factors lets the model see when two markers are really telling the same story, so that shared information is not double-counted. This directly addresses the project's earlier assumption that the factors act independently — an assumption that is convenient but known not to hold in practice.

Confidence intervals

Every figure the tool produces — a correlation, a band's diabetes prevalence, an individual's risk — is an estimate drawn from a finite sample, not a fixed truth. A confidence interval expresses how much trust to place in each number: when the cohort is small the intervals are wide, and as more confirmed results accumulate they tighten. Reporting risk as a range rather than a single percentage keeps the tool honest about what it does and does not yet know — which matters for a screening aid that is meant to prompt further testing, not deliver a verdict.

Bloodwork as a process over time

A single check-up is only a snapshot. Because the same person is tested repeatedly across their life, each marker is better understood as a quantity that drifts and fluctuates from one visit to the next — partly a steady underlying trend, partly random variation. Stochastic calculus is the branch of mathematics built for exactly this kind of randomly-evolving quantity. Viewing bloodwork through that lens lets the tool reason beyond "what is this person's risk today" towards "how is their risk trending, and how likely are they to cross a concerning threshold before their next visit" — turning an isolated reading into a trajectory. This longitudinal view is where the recalibrating design, and the value of tracking the population over time, ultimately point.

Limitations and assumptions

The team acknowledges that Type-2 Diabetes can also stem from factors the dataset does not include, such as genetics. As a result, three key types of assumptions have been identified for the project to remain valid.

Data assumptions

The data supplied is sufficient — a large enough dataset that it can be concluded to be an accurate representation of the larger Australian population. Factors within the dataset independently affect diabetes diagnosis, as no correlative coefficients were calculated between given factors.

Medical assumptions

Type-2 diabetes always manifests itself in some form of blood-related data, and thus will always be symptomatic. Type-2 diabetes is not a cause of these elevated levels; rather, it is these specific elevated blood-related levels that cause Type-2 diabetes.

Statistical assumptions

Stronger statistical relationships between factors and diabetes-diagnosis coefficients carry stronger weighting towards diabetic risk.

Key limitations
  • No removal of outliers within the dataset, which can potentially skew results.
  • The project is limited to the data provided, even when other factors may contribute to Type-2 Diabetes.
  • There are no relationships and correlations between factors, as they are assumed completely independent of one another.
  • The data collected is not from Australian individuals and may have major inaccuracies and different averages for each factor.

The project is highly promising, but is still significantly flawed. Upon reviewing the program and the outputs, a few problems associated with the project were identified as key points of discussion.

Future implementation & impact

Implementing this software for use by GPs or hospitals would be relatively straightforward. The design and optimisation of memory, storage, and the algorithm itself would likely take approximately one year. A full rollout could be achieved by the start of 2027, after which the software could continue to be developed, refined, and improved over time.

In terms of impact, it is projected that approximately 85,000 individuals will move from having undiagnosed diabetes to being diagnosed over the first three years.
